Conventional
ice cream is
empty calories
in disguise.

Traditionally engineered around sucrose and butterfat, ice cream delivers up to 280 kcal per 100 g while contributing little protein, no fiber, and no functional bioactives. For diabetic, athletic, and health-conscious consumers, it is functionally inaccessible.

Functional
ice cream is
nutrition that
still tastes
like dessert.

By replacing sucrose with stevia and erythritol, enriching with protein, and delivering live probiotic cultures inside a low-calorie matrix, ice cream becomes a vehicle for measurable health outcomes — without surrendering creaminess, flavor, or consumer pleasure.

02 — Definition Six Criteria of Functional Foods
— Framework

A food earns the
"functional" label only
when it clears six tests.

Both formulations developed in this study have been engineered to satisfy each of the criteria below — making them defensibly classifiable as functional foods, not merely "healthy desserts."

01

Basic Nutritional Value

Provides essential macronutrients — proteins, carbohydrates, fats, vitamins, minerals — supporting normal growth and maintenance.

02

Bioactive Components

Contains ingredients with proven physiological effect: protein in Product I, viable probiotic cultures in Product II.

03

Scientifically Proven Benefit

Evidence-based effects: blood-glucose modulation, satiety, microbiota balance, improved immune response.

04

Safe for Regular Use

Sweeteners (stevia, sucralose) are GRAS-classified; cultures are food-grade lyophilized strains; no excessive sugar or saturated-fat load.

05

Sensory Acceptability

Maintains creamy mouthfeel, balanced sweetness, and acceptable color — sensory parity with conventional ice cream is non-negotiable.

06

Consumed As Daily Food

Fits everyday diet patterns — not a pharmaceutical, not a supplement. Health benefit accrues through habitual consumption.

04 — Product I Why Protein Matters
— Protein, defined

An essential daily macronutrient — not optional, not seasonal.

WHO/FAO recommendation: 0.8–1.2 g of high-quality protein per kg of body weight, every day. Protein is the only macronutrient the body cannot meaningfully store — what isn't supplied today is borrowed from muscle tomorrow.

  • → Builds & repairs muscle, skin, and connective tissue
  • → Drives satiety — slows gastric emptying, blunts cravings
  • → Stabilizes blood glucose by slowing carbohydrate absorption
  • → Supplies enzymes, hormones & antibodies — immune resilience

05 — Product II Mechanism of Action
— Probiotics, defined

Live microbes, working quietly, delivering measurable outcomes.

WHO/FAO definition: "Live microorganisms which, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health benefit on the host." In a frozen dairy matrix, these organisms enter metabolic dormancy — preserving viability until the moment of consumption.

  • → Modulate gut microbiota balance
  • → Competitively exclude pathogens (E. coli, Salmonella)
  • → Strengthen intestinal barrier function
  • → Stimulate innate & adaptive immune response

— Therapeutic Minimum
CFU per gram required at the moment of consumption for a product to qualify as functionally probiotic. Our formulation maintains this threshold through full storage life.

06 — Materials & Methods Process Flow
— End-to-end manufacturing

From raw mix
to frozen functional matrix.

Six controlled stages. Each one safeguards either pathogen safety, sensory quality, or — critically — probiotic viability.

i
Standardization & Blending
≈ 40 °C · High-shear

Liquids heated, dry ingredients dispersed under agitation to prevent clumping.

ii
Pasteurization
75 °C × 15 s

Eliminates pathogens; denatures select proteins to support stabilization.

iii
Homogenization
2000 / 500 psi

Two-stage; reduces fat globule size for smooth, uniform emulsion.

vi
Freezing · Hardening
−6 °C → −25 °C

Aerated to 30–60% overrun, then blast-hardened to lock fine ice crystals.

v
Inoculation (Probiotic)
Post-pasteurization

Lyophilized cultures added before freezing — protects the live cell count.

iv
Cooling & Aging
4 °C × 4–24 h

Proteins hydrate fully; fats partially crystallize — improves final melt-down.

— Critical control points Steps ii & v are the linchpins of the entire process. Pasteurization eliminates pathogens and de-risks the matrix; post-pasteurization inoculation then preserves probiotic viability through orders of magnitude. Two small sequencing decisions, outsized functional impact.

— Takeaway

Both strains stay above the therapeutic threshold through 12 weeks of storage.

Initial counts of 10⁸ CFU/g decline gradually under cryostress, oxygen exposure, and osmotic pressure — but never breach the 10⁶ functional minimum. The product retains its functional claim through retail shelf life.

"Frozen dormancy is the friend, not the enemy, of probiotic survival."
